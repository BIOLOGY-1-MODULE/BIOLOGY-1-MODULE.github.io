What I Need to Know

Lesson 6: Cell Division (Eukaryotic vs Prokaryotic)

This supplementary module is designed to help you understand the difference between the cell division of eukaryotic and prokaryotic cell. In this lesson, you will explore how the key feature and process occurring in each stage of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cell division.

Cell division is the process by which cells reproduce to ensure growth, repair, and continuity of life. In prokaryotic cells, such as bacteria, division occurs through a simple process called binary fission, while in eukaryotic cells it undergoes a more complex process involving mitosis and cytokinesis.

While both processes result in genetically identical cells, eukaryotic cell division is more regulated and occurs in distinct stages, reflecting the greater complexity of eukaryotic cells compared to prokaryotes.

After going through this supplementary module, you are expected to:

  • Identify the stages of cell division in prokaryotic cells (binary fission) and eukaryotic cells (mitosis and cytokinesis).
  • Describe the key features and processes occurring in each stage of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cell division.
  • Compare and differentiate prokaryotic and eukaryotic cell division in terms of stages, structures involved, and overall process.

Mitosis (Eukaryotic)

Mitosis is the process by which a eukaryotic cell divides its nucleus to produce two identical sets of chromosomes. It ensures that each daughter cell receives the same genetic information. Mitosis occurs in a series of organized phases: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase, followed by cytokinesis.

Binary Fission (Prokaryotic)

Binary fission is the process by which prokaryotic cells (such as bacteria) reproduce. It is a simple, rapid form of cell division that allows one cell to divide into two genetically identical daughter cells. Unlike eukaryotic cell division, binary fission does not involve a nucleus or complex stages like mitosis. Instead, it follows a direct and efficient sequence of events:

  • The process begins with DNA replication, where the single circular chromosome of the prokaryotic cell is copied. This results in two identical DNA molecules.
  • Next is cell elongation, during which the cell increases in size. As the cell grows longer, the two copies of DNA begin to move apart within the cytoplasm.
  • In the third stage, DNA moves to opposite ends of the cell. Each copy of the chromosome attaches to different parts of the cell membrane and is actively separated.
  • Following this is septum formation, where the cell membrane begins to pinch inward at the center. A structure called the septum forms, gradually dividing the cell into two compartments.
  • Finally, the cell undergoes cell division, where the septum fully forms and the cell splits into two identical daughter cells.

Overall, binary fission is an efficient and continuous cycle that enables prokaryotic organisms to reproduce quickly, often leading to rapid population growth under favorable conditions.
