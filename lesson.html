Module 1

What is a Cell?

A cell is the smallest structural and functional unit of all living organisms. Every living thing you see around you — from a tiny blade of grass to a giant blue whale, including yourself — is made entirely of cells.

Some organisms are made of only one single cell (we call these unicellular organisms, like bacteria). Others, like humans, are made of trillions of cells working together (multicellular organisms).

💡 Did you know? The human body contains an estimated 30 to 40 trillion cells. There are over 200 different types of cells in your body, each with a specific job, like carrying oxygen or fighting off viruses!

Cells are incredibly busy. They carry out all the processes necessary for life: they take in nutrients, convert those nutrients into energy, carry out specialized functions, and reproduce as necessary. Understanding the cell is the absolute foundation of all biology.

The Historic Scientists

The discovery of cells wasn't a one-day event. Because cells are (usually) too small to be seen with the naked eye, their discovery had to wait for the invention of the microscope. Over nearly two centuries, four key scientists pieced together the puzzle of life.

Module 3

The Cell Theory

By combining the observations of Hooke, Leeuwenhoek, Schleiden, Schwann (who did for animals what Schleiden did for plants), and Virchow, science arrived at a unified concept.

The Cell Theory is one of the foundational principles of biology. It consists of three core tenets:

  1. All living organisms are composed of one or more cells.
  2. The cell is the basic unit of structure and organization in organisms.
  3. Cells arise only from pre-existing cells.

This theory revolutionized medicine and science. Understanding that diseases affect cells — and that life doesn't just spontaneously appear from thin air — helped doctors figure out how infections spread and how the body heals itself.
